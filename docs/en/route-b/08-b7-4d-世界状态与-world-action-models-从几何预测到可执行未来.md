# Learning Objectives and Scope

After completing this course, readers should be able to distinguish among action-conditioned video models, joint World Action Models, 4D geometric world models, physical world models, and controllers; formulate the joint distribution and training objective of a WAM; explain the key designs of WAM4D and X-WAM; and design closed-loop experiments that test imagination–action consistency.

**Prerequisites:** Readers are advised to first complete B1 State Spaces and Latent Dynamics, B2 Model-Based Planning, B4 Video World Models, and B6 Modern Embodied World Models. Familiarity with conditional probability, diffusion or Flow Matching, camera geometry, depth maps, and Action Chunks is required.

**Scope:** In this course, 4D means 3D space evolving over time, namely 3D + time. It is not a fourth spatial dimension, nor does it automatically include mass, friction, contact forces, stiffness, actuator dynamics, or material deformation. 4D geometric consistency is an important condition for physical modeling, but it is not sufficient for complete physical modeling.

# 1. What Does a World Action Model Actually Model Jointly?

A WAM is not simply a model with both a video head and an action head. Its core purpose is to enable information exchange between future world representations and executable actions within the same conditional model, allowing it to answer both “What will this action cause?” and “What action should be taken to reach this future?”

## 1.1 The Joint Probability Object

$$p_\theta(o_{t+1:t+H},a_{t:t+H-1}\mid h_t,g)$$

> **Interpretation:** Given the history h_t up to the current time and task condition g, the model assigns a joint probability to observations and Action Chunks over the next H steps.

**Derivation:** If future observations and actions are generated by the same latent interaction process, they cannot be treated merely as two unrelated supervised heads. Joint modeling allows action tokens to constrain the future world and future-world representations to constrain action generation in return.

The joint distribution can be factorized with actions first:

$$p_\theta(a_{t:t+H-1}\mid h_t,g)\,p_\theta(o_{t+1:t+H}\mid h_t,g,a_{t:t+H-1})$$

> **Interpretation:** First generate actions from the history and goal, and then predict future observations conditioned on those actions.

It can also be factorized with the future first:

$$p_\theta(o_{t+1:t+H}\mid h_t,g)\,p_\theta(a_{t:t+H-1}\mid h_t,g,o_{t+1:t+H})$$

> **Interpretation:** First represent a possible or desired future, and then recover the actions needed to realize that future through an inverse-dynamics-style action head.

These two factorizations correspond to different generation orders and control interfaces. What must actually be examined is not which name a paper uses, but whether a trainable, intervenable, and evaluable consistency constraint exists between actions and futures.

## 1.2 “Looking Predictively Accurate” Does Not Mean “The Action Is Correct”

Video losses may reward textures, backgrounds, and average motion trends, whereas action losses require control quantities to be correct in the robot coordinate frame, timing, and execution frequency. Sharing representations may allow the two objectives to reinforce each other, but it may also cause gradient conflicts. BadWAM further demonstrates that even when a predicted future looks plausible, world–action drift may still occur under perturbations. The minimum unit of evidence for a WAM must therefore include all three elements—actions, imagined futures, and real execution outcomes—rather than video quality alone.

# 2. A 4D Representation Is Not a Format but a Set of Design Choices

“4D” can be instantiated in different representation spaces. The choice of representation determines what the model can perceive, how losses are defined, how expensive inference is, and whether a planner can directly access collision and reachability information.

| Representation | Information Preserved | Main Advantage | Key Gap |
|-|-|-|-|
| Single-view RGB video | Appearance and 2D motion | Can reuse large-scale video priors | Depth, occluded structure, and scale are ambiguous |
| Multi-view RGB | Cross-view appearance constraints | Reduces single-view ambiguity | Geometry remains implicit, and stringent view synchronization is required |
| RGB-D / depth sequences | Per-frame surface distance | Enables reprojection and spatial-error computation | Occluded regions, transparent or reflective objects, and sensor noise remain challenging |
| Point clouds and Scene Flow | 3D positions and motion fields | Suitable for analyzing object motion and contact neighborhoods | Point correspondence, topology changes, and long-horizon accumulated errors remain substantial |
| Occupancy / SDF | Occupancy, free space, or implicit surfaces | Facilitates collision checking and planning | High-resolution decoding is expensive, and semantics and dynamics may still be insufficient |
| Dynamic NeRF / 4D Gaussian | Time-varying radiance fields under continuous viewpoints | High-quality novel-view synthesis | Weak support for online updates, action causality, and real-time control interfaces |
| Object-centric states | Object poses, velocities, relations, and events | Interpretable and suitable for task planning | Object discovery and non-rigid interactions are prone to distortion |
| Spatial Register Tokens | Compressed geometric supervision and spatial priors | Avoids dense 4D decoding during execution | Ablation studies are needed to verify whether spatial information adequately enters the action representation |

A 4D-WAM should therefore not be classified solely by whether it outputs depth. Three more important questions are whether geometric information is used during training or inference; whether geometry is densely decoded or compressed into tokens; and whether the geometry branch actually improves action success rates rather than merely improving offline reconstruction metrics.

# 3. From Video Priors to Joint Action and Geometry Models

Modern WAMs often begin with a pretrained video diffusion Transformer or video generation model because video models have already absorbed visual priors such as object motion, occlusion, and appearance changes before and after contact. Robotic data then provides actions, proprioceptive states, task conditions, and geometric supervision.

## 3.1 Multi-Objective Training

$$\mathcal{L}=\lambda_v\mathcal{L}_{video}+\lambda_a\mathcal{L}_{action}+\lambda_g\mathcal{L}_{geometry}+\lambda_c\mathcal{L}_{consistency}$$

> **Interpretation:** The total loss is a weighted combination of four objective categories: video generation, action prediction, geometric supervision, and action–future consistency.

**Derivation:** The video term provides appearance and temporal priors, the action term anchors the representation to executable control, the geometry term penalizes spatial inconsistency, and the consistency term requires generated actions and imagined outcomes to explain each other. The weights should not be determined solely from the numerical scales of the losses; they should also be established through ablations of action success rates, geometric errors, and gradient conflicts.

## 3.2 Causal Visibility and Information Leakage

During training, the model may simultaneously observe future video, depth, and actions. If the attention mask allows action tokens to directly access future information that would be unavailable during execution, offline action errors may look excellent while deployment performance collapses. It is therefore necessary to specify which times and modalities each type of token can access during training and inference, and to verify that the action head remains causal after future supervision is removed.

## 3.3 Synchronous and Asynchronous Generation

Video generally requires more denoising steps to achieve high fidelity, whereas control actions must be produced with low latency. Synchronous denoising gives both modalities a unified schedule but slows action generation; fully decoupling them may instead break the joint distribution. Asynchronous methods attempt to align different noise times during training while allowing the action head to finish early during inference as the world branch continues to refine its output.

# 4. WAM4D: Injecting Geometric Priors with Spatial Register Tokens

WAM4D starts from the observation that even when a 2D video WAM is visually plausible, it may ignore the 3D spatial constraints and occluded contact geometry required for fine manipulation. However, densely generating a complete 4D scene at every control cycle would be too slow.

**Spatial Register Tokens.** WAM4D uses lightweight spatial register tokens for future-depth readout during training, distilling or transferring spatial priors from a pretrained geometry model into a causal video–action Transformer. At execution time, the register branch can be removed, retaining only the action pathway that has absorbed the spatial supervision and thereby avoiding dense geometric decoding.

**Causal Mixture Attention.** The model defines different visibility rules for video, action, and geometry tokens, preventing future depth or future video from leaking into the action head through non-causal shortcuts. The value of this design is not merely that it “uses MoT,” but that it explicitly defines the information permissions of different modalities along the time axis.

**Course Assessment.** If removing depth supervision reduces action success rates without significantly increasing execution latency, this supports the conclusion that “geometric priors improve action representations.” However, it still does not prove that the model has learned contact forces or material properties. Conditions involving transparent objects, occluded grasps, camera extrinsic perturbations, and recovery from contact failures must be examined further.

**Paper:** [WAM4D: Fast 4D World Action Model via Spatial Register Tokens](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.14048)

# 5. X-WAM: Multi-View RGB-D and Asynchronous Denoising

X-WAM places real-time action execution and high-fidelity 4D world synthesis in the same framework. It uses a pretrained video diffusion model to imagine future multi-view RGB-D video and creates a dedicated depth branch by duplicating the final several DiT blocks, recovering future spatial information with relatively minor architectural changes.

## 5.1 Asynchronous Noise Sampling

Actions and video have different requirements for denoising quality. During training, X-WAM’s asynchronous noise sampling draws from a joint distribution over action and world timesteps, allowing the model to adapt to the asynchronous schedule used at inference time: the action branch decodes rapidly with fewer steps, while the video and depth branches continue through additional denoising steps.

$$t_a\sim q_a(t),\quad t_w\sim q_w(t),\quad (t_a,t_w)\sim q_{joint}$$

> **Interpretation:** The action noise time and world noise time may differ, but they are not sampled completely independently; instead, they are drawn from a joint schedule consistent with the inference process.

**Why It Matters:** If actions and video are always trained at the same noise level, terminating action denoising early during inference causes distribution shift. If they are sampled entirely independently, alignment between actions and the future world may be weakened. Joint sampling is a compromise between latency and consistency.

**Evidence and Limitations:** The paper reports results on RoboCasa, RoboTwin 2.0, and pretraining with more than 5,800 hours of robotic data, while evaluating both action success rates and 4D generation quality. Real-robot control frequency, multi-view calibration errors, depth-branch cost, and failure recovery should still be verified separately.

**Paper:** [Unified 4D World Action Modeling from Video Priors with Asynchronous Denoising](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.26694)

# 6. The Latest WAM Directions in 2026

As of July 2026, the WAM frontier has expanded beyond “jointly generating video and actions” to encompass new control interfaces, long-term memory, test-time computation, and safety attack surfaces. The following sections are organized by mechanism rather than by company.

## 6.1 Masked Visual Actions: Turning Actions into Trajectory Conditions in Visual Space

Masked Visual Actions represent an action as a partial trajectory of an entity in a video. When robot motion is revealed, the model performs forward-dynamics prediction of the scene response; when target-object motion is revealed, the same model infers the robot behavior required to achieve that outcome. This provides a pixel-space control interface across embodiments and allows forward prediction, candidate-future ranking, and inverse modeling to share a single checkpoint.

[Masked Visual Actions for Unified World Modeling](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.19343)

## 6.2 WorldScape Policy 2.0: Memory, Events, and Controllable Long-Horizon Planning

WorldScape Policy 2.0 uses short-term visual memory as a DiT prefill while organizing historical VLM outputs into global history, local activity, and event-boundary memories. Retrieved results enhance perception and autoregressive planning tokens, forming implicit subgoals; event-level text, goal images, and video context provide finer-grained control. It represents the progression of WAMs from short Action Chunks toward task-progress tracking and long-horizon planning.

[WorldScape Policy 2.0](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.18840)

## 6.3 Geometric-Consistency-Driven Test-Time Scaling

Because a single WAM sample exposes both actions and predicted futures, the model can generate multiple candidate rollouts and perform Best-of-N selection using cross-view depth reprojection consistency computed by a frozen geometry model. Selective gating adds computation only when the initial rollout is internally inconsistent, demonstrating that an “imagined future” can serve as a signal for allocating inference budgets rather than merely as a visualization by-product.

[Test-Time Scaling for World Action Models via Zero-Shot Geometric Evaluation](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.17454)

## 6.4 BadWAM: Imagining Correctly but Acting Incorrectly

BadWAM investigates world–action drift: small visual perturbations can significantly alter actions while leaving predicted futures close to those imagined from clean inputs. This refutes the assumption that plausible future imagery inherently guarantees safe actions and requires evaluations to report action drift, imagination drift, task failures, and attack visibility separately.

[BadWAM: When World-Action Models Dream Right but Act Wrong](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15207)

## 6.5 Cross-Embodiment Expansion

AeroAct applies a WAM to language-conditioned quadrotor flight, using future egocentric video as consequence supervision during training and directly decoding trajectory actions at deployment. GeoWorldAD introduces current and future geometry tokens into autonomous-driving trajectory refinement. These studies show that WAMs are expanding from robotic-arm manipulation to systems with different dynamics and timescales, but cross-domain results cannot be treated directly as evidence of general-purpose robotic capability.

# 7. Boundaries Among 4D-WAMs, Physical World Models, and Whole-Body Control

| System | Primary Output | Question Answered | What It Cannot Replace |
|-|-|-|-|
| 4D-WAM | Actions, future video, depth, or spatial tokens | How can actions and time-varying spatial outcomes be predicted jointly? | Complete contact dynamics and stable control |
| Latent world model | Control-relevant latent states, rewards, and values | How can efficient rollout and planning be performed? | Interpretable 3D geometry, unless explicitly incorporated |
| Physical world model | States, forces, contact modes, and uncertainty | Do changes satisfy dynamics and material constraints? | High-level semantic planning and open-vocabulary task understanding |
| VLA / action policy | Action Chunks or trajectories | What action should be executed now? | Explicit future prediction and stable actuator-level feedback control |
| Whole-Body Control | Joint targets, torques, and contact forces | How can balance, contact, and task constraints be satisfied? | Long-horizon semantic reasoning and visual world imagination |

A practical robotic system is typically a hierarchical closed loop: a WAM or VLA provides short-horizon actions, visual subgoals, candidate futures, and uncertainty; a trajectory optimizer or MPC converts them into constrained trajectories; and a Whole-Body Controller handles balance, contacts, torque limits, and disturbance recovery at high frequency.

$$g_t,\hat{o}_{t+1:t+H},\Sigma_t\rightarrow \text{planner}\rightarrow q^{ref},\dot q^{ref},f^{ref}\rightarrow \text{WBC}\rightarrow \tau_t$$

> **Interpretation:** The WAM outputs subgoals, predicted futures, and uncertainty; the planner generates reference joint states and contact forces; and the whole-body controller ultimately outputs real-time torques.

Even if a WAM directly outputs joint actions, safety filtering, interpolation, saturation handling, and low-level feedback are still required. The prediction frequency of a world model must not be conflated with the execution frequency of a controller.

# 8. How to Evaluate Whether a WAM Truly Understands Action Consequences

WAM evaluation must separate at least four levels: world prediction, action quality, consistency between the two, and real closed-loop performance.

| Evidence Level | Recommended Metrics | Common Misinterpretation |
|-|-|-|
| Visual future | Perceptual distance, temporal consistency, object-state changes | Sharp imagery is equivalent to physical correctness |
| 4D geometry | Depth error, cross-view reprojection, Scene Flow, collision and penetration rates | Accurate single-frame depth is equivalent to long-horizon geometric stability |
| Actions | Action error, trajectory smoothness, latency, control frequency | Low offline behavior-cloning error is equivalent to closed-loop success |
| World–Action consistency | Consistency between predicted outcomes and action directions, counterfactual ranking, cycle consistency | A shared backbone produces automatic alignment |
| Real closed loop | Success rate, recovery rate, collision rate, intervention rate, long-horizon task completion rate | Average simulator scores can replace real-world intervention testing |
| Uncertainty and safety | Calibration, risk coverage, drift under perturbations, rejection and fallback behavior | Visualized imagination inherently provides a safety guarantee |

## 8.1 Required Intervention Experiments

- Hold the observation fixed and change the action: does the future object state change in the direction of the action?
- Hold the action fixed and change the object position or camera: does the action adjust appropriately to the geometric relationship?
- Remove geometric supervision: does the success-rate improvement actually come from the spatial branch?
- Shuffle action–video pairings: does the model depend on genuine causal correspondence rather than dataset backgrounds?
- Introduce cross-view occlusion: do invisible contact regions remain consistent rather than exhibiting penetration?
- Perform long-horizon rollouts: do correct single-step predictions accumulate into geometric drift after multiple steps?
- Add small visual perturbations: do imagination and action exhibit BadWAM-style decoupling?

# 9. Minimal Reproducible Experiment

**Experimental Task:** In a dual-view tabletop pushing or pick-and-place environment, the model is given a target object position and outputs an Action Chunk of 8 to 16 steps while predicting future RGB-D from both views.

## 9.1 Four Baseline Models

1. Action-only: predicts actions only.
2. Video + Action: shares representations but does not use explicit geometry.
3. RGB-D + Action: adds future-depth supervision.
4. 4D Token + Action: uses spatial tokens during training and removes dense decoding during execution.

## 9.2 Data and Splits

The training data must include camera timestamps, extrinsic-calibration versions, proprioceptive states, action windows, object states, and success labels. The test set should be stratified by object instance, initial position, degree of occlusion, and camera perturbation to prevent trajectory leakage caused by random frame-level splitting.

## 9.3 Results That Must Be Reported

Report action error, closed-loop success rate, depth and reprojection errors, collision or penetration rate, per-decision latency, and GPU memory usage together. Then perform ablations on depth supervision, causal masks, asynchronous schedules, and the number of candidate rollouts. If geometric metrics improve but the success rate does not, the conclusion should be written as “improves spatial prediction,” not “improves control capability.”

## 9.4 Counterfactual Testing

For the same initial observation, input four groups of actions—left, right, toward, and away—and examine the sign and magnitude of the future object displacement. Then, given the same target future, determine whether the inverse action remains geometrically equivalent across camera viewpoints. This experiment tests action causality more directly than computing video-generation metrics alone.

# 11. Lesson Conclusions and Review Questions

The value of 4D-WAM lies not in adding a depth head to a video model, but in organizing actions, future world states, and spatial constraints into prediction targets that can be cross-validated. WAM4D demonstrates a trade-off between geometric supervision during training and lightweight action inference; X-WAM demonstrates multi-view RGB-D world generation and asynchronous action decoding; more recent work further advances WAMs toward visual action interfaces, long-term memory, test-time scaling, and safety auditing.

However, the boundaries of these conclusions must be kept clear: correct 3D geometry does not imply correct contact dynamics, plausible future frames do not imply safe actions, and joint training does not mean that the two outputs are inherently consistent. A WAM can be considered a world-action model suitable for control only when counterfactual interventions, geometric consistency, action latency, real-world closed-loop operation, and failure recovery are all validated.

## Review Questions

1. Why is 3D + time still insufficient to fully represent friction, contact forces, and material properties?
2. What control interfaces are best suited to action-first and future-first WAM factorizations, respectively?
3. Why can WAM4D remove the geometry readout branch at execution time? What ablations are needed to demonstrate that spatial information is still incorporated into the action representation?
4. What training–inference distribution shift does X-WAM's asynchronous noise sampling address?
5. Why does BadWAM demonstrate that “plausible imagination” is not a sufficient condition for safety?
6. Design a counterfactual experiment that holds observations fixed while varying actions, and specify the success criteria.
7. How should a 4D-WAM, MPC, and a whole-body controller divide responsibilities in a humanoid robot system?
